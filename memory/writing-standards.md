# Writing Standards — the house style

Distilled from the desk standards of the New York Times, Wall Street Journal, Bloomberg, The Economist, The Atlantic, and ProPublica. The `write` phase of the digital-journalist workflow loads this file. Update it when a technique proves to land (or fail).

The goal: an article that reads like it was reported and written by a senior journalist at a top-tier desk — authoritative, evidence-dense, fair, and a pleasure to read.

---

## 1. The structure (WSJ "formula" + Bloomberg discipline)

The Wall Street Journal feature formula, adapted:

1. **The lede (1–2 short paragraphs).** Open on a *specific* — a person, a scene, a striking number, a concrete moment. Never open with abstraction ("Education policy is complex…"). Make the reader feel something is at stake. NYT calls this the "ladder of abstraction": start on the bottom rung (concrete), climb to the general.
2. **The nut graf (paragraph 2–4).** The "so what" paragraph. State plainly what this piece is about, why it matters *now*, and what's at stake. A reader who stops here should still know the point. Bloomberg's discipline: the reader should grasp the news and its significance within the first 60 words.
3. **The body.** Evidence in descending order of importance (inverted pyramid for the core facts), then thematic sections with subheads. Each section advances the argument with quotes, data, and named sources. Alternate evidence types: a stat, then a human voice, then context.
4. **The turn / the contrarian section.** Steelman the strongest opposing view. Quote the most articulate critic *by name and at length*. Then engage it with evidence — don't dismiss it.
5. **The kicker.** End on a strong, forward-looking line or a resonant quote. Never end with a flat summary ("In conclusion…"). The kicker should echo the lede or open a window.

For shorter/newsier pieces: pure inverted pyramid — most important fact first, details in descending order.

## 2. The lede — what good looks like

- **Anecdotal lede:** a real person facing the stakes. ("When Maria Chen's eighth-grade son was told he couldn't take algebra, she did the math herself: by graduation he'd be two years behind…")
- **Scene lede:** a vivid moment, present tense.
- **Surprising-fact lede:** a single counterintuitive number or fact, then unpack it.
- **Avoid:** the dictionary lede, the rhetorical-question lede, the "since the dawn of time" lede, the "we live in a world where" lede.

## 3. Attribution — cite or it doesn't exist

- Every fact, statistic, and characterization is attributed. "According to a 2023 Stanford study [3]…" not "studies show."
- Name sources and give their title on first reference: "Thomas Dee, a Stanford education economist [5]…"
- Quotes carry full attribution: `— Name, Title, Publication, YYYY-MM-DD`.
- Distinguish what's *known* (documented), *claimed* (asserted by a source), and *disputed* (sources disagree).
- Prefer primary sources. If a news article cites a report, cite the report.

## 4. Quotes vs. paraphrase

- **Quote** for opinion, voice, color, and contested characterizations — when *how* something was said matters.
- **Paraphrase** for plain facts — don't quote "The policy started in 2014" when you can state it.
- Never quote the obvious or the bureaucratic. Cut "We are committed to student success."
- Use a quote to let a *person* carry an argument the data supports.

## Source & quote hierarchy (credibility-first) — NON-NEGOTIABLE

Readers don't trust numbers from random sites and bloggers. Lead with the sources that survive scrutiny; reach for weaker ones only when nothing stronger exists.

**Cite most-credible-first.** For any claim, use the strongest available source, in this order:
1. **Primary / official** — government statistics & agencies (BLS, Census, the Federal Reserve and its regional banks, CBO, GAO, SEC/EDGAR filings, FTC, OECD, IMF, World Bank, Eurostat, central banks & national statistics offices) and official disclosures (10-K, earnings calls, regulator filings, court records).
2. **Peer-reviewed / academic** — journals, NBER, university research centers; arXiv marked "preprint."
3. **Market research & consulting** — Gartner, Forrester, IDC, McKinsey, BCG, Bain, Deloitte, PwC, Accenture, CB Insights, PitchBook, Statista, eMarketer, Nielsen. Name the firm + report + date; note their commercial incentive.
4. **Established journalism** — NYT, WSJ, FT, Reuters, AP, Bloomberg, The Economist, The Atlantic, ProPublica.
5. **Everything else** — Substack, personal blogs, social posts. Use to *surface* arguments and leads, then verify against 1–4. **A Tier 4–5 source must never carry a load-bearing fact.** Quote one only when the author is themselves the authority on the point.

When you're forced onto a weaker source, say so in the prose ("according to one widely-shared but unverified account…") rather than laundering it as established fact.

**Quote most-authoritative-first.** Prefer quotes from, in order:
1. The **named decision-maker / authority** — a CEO or senior executive of a major organization, a government official, an agency head.
2. A **frequently-cited academic or the named author** of the primary study being discussed.
3. A **recognized analyst** at a named research/consulting firm.
4. An **anonymous or "regular" individual** — used **only** for lived-experience color, explicitly framed as one person's anecdote, *never* as evidence for a general claim.

A vivid quote from a recognizable name earns trust a paraphrase can't. Spend the reader's attention on voices they already credit; use the everyman quote to humanize, not to prove.

**Triangulate with comparables.** For any company or industry claim, check peer/competitor companies (their filings, results, public statements) so a single firm's self-report isn't the whole basis.

## 5. Numbers — context, not dumps

- Always contextualize: "a 15% drop — from 1,100 students to 935" beats "a 15% drop."
- Convert to human scale: per-student, per-year, per-capita.
- One vivid number beats five vague ones. Don't stat-dump.
- Round sensibly; precision implies confidence you may not have.
- Flag the date of every statistic. Old data gets "as of 2021."

## 6. Voice and sentence craft

- **Active voice.** "The board cut the program," not "the program was cut."
- **Plain words.** "use" not "utilize," "show" not "demonstrate," "help" not "facilitate."
- **Vary sentence length.** A short punch after two longer sentences. Like that.
- **No throat-clearing.** Cut "It is important to note that," "Needless to say," "At the end of the day."
- **Show, don't tell.** Replace adjectives with evidence. Not "a controversial policy" — show the controversy.
- **Kill clichés and AI-slop tells:** "delve," "tapestry," "navigate the landscape," "in today's fast-paced world," "it's worth noting," "stands as a testament," "plays a crucial role," "the world of."

## 7. Fairness (the steelman rule)

- Give the opposing side its strongest case, in the words of its best advocate.
- If evidence favors one side, say so — but only *after* representing the other fairly. "Both sides" is not neutrality when the evidence is lopsided; weigh it.
- Disclose your sources' incentives and funding when relevant (follow-the-money lens feeds this).

## 8. The "butter to the recipe" test (project-specific, non-negotiable)

Before declaring done:
- Does the piece say something **no single source said**? If not, synthesize harder.
- Is there a **number, name, or date** the reader can quote at dinner?
- Did you **steelman the contrarian** before engaging it?
- Could a skeptic **dismantle a key claim with one click**? If so, verify or qualify.
- Is there an honest **"What we don't know"** section?

## 9. Headlines & SEO (for the titles deliverable)

Ten suggested titles must:
- **Match search intent** — phrase it how a real person Googles it ("why doesn't SF teach algebra in middle school" → "Why San Francisco Stopped Teaching Algebra in Middle School").
- **Front-load the primary keyword.**
- **Be specific** — concrete nouns, a year or number where it fits.
- **Differ from every source headline** — check `sources.md`; don't echo an existing title.
- **Earn the click honestly** — compelling, not clickbait. No "You won't believe…"
- Offer a mix: 3–4 SEO/search-intent-led, 3–4 editorial/curiosity-led, 2–3 provocative/contrarian.
- Keep most under ~60 characters for SERP display; a couple can run longer for editorial use.

## 10. Length discipline

1,500–3,000 words for a feature. Every paragraph earns its place: a fact, a quote, or a synthesis the reader didn't have. If a section can be cut without losing insight, cut it. Insight density > word count.
